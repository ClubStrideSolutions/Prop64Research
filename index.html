Strategic Recommendations (UPDATED)

Priority 1: Implement Pre/Post Workshop Assessments (URGENT)

Most Critical Gap: Even with local baseline data, we cannot measure workshop effectiveness without pre/post assessments. Design and implement immediately:
  1. Pre-test (before workshop): Knowledge of marijuana harms, perceived ease of access, past 30-day use (confidential), intentions to use
  2. Post-test (immediately after): Knowledge gains, changed perceptions
  3. Follow-up (30-90 days): Self-reported use, decision-making scenarios

Priority 2: Focus on Grade 9 Transition

Data confirms Grade 9 as the critical intervention point - substance use nearly doubles from Grade 7 (7%) to Grade 9 (13%). Intensify prevention programming during 8th-9th grade transition to interrupt escalation pattern.

Priority 3: Intensive Services for Non-Traditional Students

Non-Traditional students show 2.7x higher AOD use (32% vs 12%) and 4x higher near-daily marijuana use (16% vs 4%). Replicate John Finney's success model: small class sizes, intensive counseling, trauma-informed practices.

Priority 4: Mental Health Integration

Students with chronic sadness show elevated substance use. Implement integrated mental health and substance use screening and intervention, rather than treating these issues separately.

Priority 5: School Connectedness Building

Only 33-40% of students feel connected to school. Increase school connectedness to protective threshold of >50% through student voice initiatives, meaningful participation opportunities, and relationship-building.

5. Youth Victimization & Substance Use Connection (NEW INSIGHT)

The Violence-Substance Use Nexus

New Vallejo data reveals overlapping crises: 39.6% of 7th graders experienced bullying, 41% were physically assaulted, and 30% feared being beaten up - while simultaneously, 7% are already using AOD. By Grade 11, victimization decreases but substance use has tripled to 19%. This suggests that middle school violence exposure may drive later substance use as a coping mechanism.

Intervention Implication: The inverse relationship between victimization (highest in middle school) and substance use (highest in high school) suggests that trauma from middle school bullying/violence may drive later substance use. Programs must integrate trauma-informed approaches with violence prevention AND substance use prevention, recognizing them as connected rather than separate issues.
